In 2014 Kurando Mitsutake presented Gun Woman, a Japanese grindhouse action film starring Kairi Narita and ex AV actress Asami Sugiura. It follows the tale of a doctor whose life was destroyed when the millionaire son of one of his earlier patients, abducts him and his wife Keiko _(Midori Okada)_ to make him pay for not being able to save his father. The doctor is left crippled and traumatized after the man tortures them both, forcing him to witness the rape and murder of his beloved wife. After barely surviving the incident, the doctor swears to take revenge on the man, and ellaborates a plan that involves training a broken woman to force her into becoming the killer machine he needs to get his payback.

<a href="https://ibb.co/xtk6xs7m"><img src="https://i.ibb.co/wFk45LzJ/IMG-COM-202506030030169051.webp" alt="IMG-COM-202506030030169051" border="0"></a>

Gun Woman is a simple but entertaining take on revenge stories. It’s rough around the edges, sure, but it manages to stay engaging throughout its runtime. The director's choice of presenting the story as a tale recounted through flashbacks is a good decision that helps cover for some of the plot omissions that the film doesn’t fully develop, and helps to keep the progression of the events natural and not come across as disjointed. Despite that, the biggest issue to me is that there's really not much that actually happens during the film, the buildup to the action is mostly a long and pretty slow training montage, and many things that are shown in between aren't explored at all. A good example of this is "The Room", the necrophilliac club run by elites the billionaire frequented, we don't get an explanation on how did this place came to be or what happened to it after the showdown that occurs in it, neither how did our doctor get his contacts in the underground world to get close to this place. Many other interesting elements the movie hints at where left unexplored, which feels like a wasted opportunity to have enriched the plot. In regards to the action splatterhouse fest the trailers and posters suggested the movie would be, I feel it under-delivers in their promises. There's not much fighting or gore happening almost till the end of the movie,
, which can feel rewarding if you've been engaged with the characters and their motivations, but a bit underwhelming if you came in expecting to see more. Most of the movie sets up what feels like a wildly dangerous and impossible mission, but when it finally plays out, the actual threat doesn’t hit quite as hard as the buildup suggests. its pacing was a big issue to me,

<a href="https://ibb.co/sJcvtLjp"><img src="https://i.ibb.co/84GDXFMn/IMG-COM-202505200149173808.webp" alt="IMG-COM-202505200149173808" border="0"></a>

 Visually, the cinematography is pretty straightforward most of the time. Some shots are genuinely stylish, while in others, the low budget becomes more apparent. That said, it’s clear the team behind the camera did the most with what they had. The practical effects are convincing enough, but strangely, during the final scenes where you’d expect the most carnage the quality drops and the bleeding becomes oddly restrained, almost like they ran out of fake blood when it mattered most. The editing also starts showing some not-too-subtle cuts during the last action moments; these aren’t overly distracting, but they do take a bit of the punch out of the climax if you’re paying close attention.

<a href="https://ibb.co/G4RqHLF1"><img src="https://i.ibb.co/YFhx0MbK/IMG-COM-202505200149173806.webp" alt="IMG-COM-202505200149173806" border="0"></a>

The acting is also a bit of a mixed bag. The Japanese cast delivers the strongest performances overall, especially Asami showcasing an impressive physicality in the choreographies. Another standout is Noriaki Kamata’s with his portrayal of the rapist, he makes the character feel genuinely unsettling with a demeanor that seems inspired by real-life infamous cannibal Issei Sagawa. In contrast, some of the American actors and extras feel stiff or under-directed in some scenes, as if they didn’t have the luxury of multiple takes or enough rehearsal.

Overall, Gun Woman is pretty entertaining if you’re okay with something reminiscent of Steven Seagal's action flicks, it’s over-the-top at times, but self-aware and quite entertaining. The effort is clearly there, and I had a good time with it. I’d rate it a solid 7.
